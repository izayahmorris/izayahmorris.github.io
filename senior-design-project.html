Laboratory-Scale Wind Tunnel

A functioning educational wind tunnel for fluid mechanics research and aerodynamic testing

Fluid Mechanics CAD Design 3D Printing Data Analysis Team Project

Project Overview

This senior design project involved developing a functional laboratory-scale wind tunnel for integration into Doane University's Engineering curriculum. As part of a four-person team, I contributed to the design, fabrication, and testing of a complete wind tunnel system capable of generating controlled air velocities for aerodynamic research and educational demonstrations.

Team Members: Matthew Arent, Izayah Morris, Zack Perez, Mason Zimmerman

Completion Date: May 2024

Purpose: Create an educational wind tunnel enabling students to conduct fluid mechanics experiments, analyze drag forces, and understand aerodynamic principles through hands-on testing and data collection.

System Specifications & Requirements

Performance Specifications

  • Air Velocity Range: 30-60 mph (13-27 m/s)
  • Control Method: Variable transformer (Variac)
  • Power Supply: 120V, 20A standard outlet
  • Test Section: 6.5" × 6.5" square
  • Turbulence Level: Below 0.2% for quality R&D

Key Components

  • 16" diameter tube-axial fan (3284 CFM max)
  • PolyWall intake and diffuser sections
  • 3D-printed adapter components
  • Plexiglass observation windows
  • Vernier force sensor & LabQuest data acquisition

Development Phases

Phase I: Alpha Prototype

The initial prototype was constructed using readily available materials from the engineering lab: cardboard diffuser with wooden support frames, plexiglass test section (7" × 7") with metal angle iron bracing, and a PolyWall intake. Testing at this stage revealed critical issues with structural integrity—the cardboard diffuser collapsed under vacuum pressure, and turbulent flow developed from internal wooden frame supports.

Phase II: Beta Prototype

Based on Alpha phase findings, we redesigned the diffuser as a cylindrical PolyWall component (9.25" to 16" diameter transition) to better match the fan shape and reduce eddy formation. The test section was optimized for the Vernier sensor setup, and 3D-printed adapters were designed to create smooth transitions between components. This prototype demonstrated significantly improved flow characteristics and yielded experimental data closely matching theoretical predictions.

Phase III: Final Prototype

The final iteration incorporated all lessons learned from previous phases. The test section was redesigned as a three-piece assembly with precision-dimensioned components, improved plexiglass supports with dowel connections, and a dedicated testing rod assembly with refined moment arm design. The system achieved optimal flow laminarization and accurate force measurements.

CAD Design & Engineering

All components were modeled in SolidWorks to ensure precise fit and assembly, with particular attention to adapter transitions and mounting points. Key design considerations included:

  • Geometric Design & Tolerancing (GD&T) for critical dimensions
  • Press-fit and tolerance specifications for component assembly
  • Laminar flow optimization through radius transitions and smooth surfaces
  • Structural finite element analysis for component strength validation
  • 3D printing orientation and support structure design

Design Achievement

Successfully created detailed 3D assemblies of all components with GD&T specifications. Developed custom 3D-printed adapters that maintained laminar flow characteristics while seamlessly connecting circular (diffuser) to square (test section) geometries. The iterative design process allowed simulation of flow patterns before physical manufacturing, reducing material waste and development time.

Testing & Data Analysis

Velocity Characterization

We employed both anemometer and Pitot tube methods to characterize air velocity across the test section at various voltage settings (60V to 120V). The anemometer testing provided direct wind speed measurements, while Pitot tube testing validated results using pressure differential equations.

Drag Force Testing

Drag force measurements were conducted using a Vernier dual-range force sensor (0.01-50N range) with LabQuest data acquisition interface. Testing objects included a golf ball and a 3D-printed sphere to validate theoretical drag coefficient models.

Theoretical Drag Force Equation: FD = ½ ρv²CDA

  • ρ = Air density (1.204 kg/m³)
  • v = Velocity (m/s)
  • CD = Drag coefficient (0.47 for sphere)
  • A = Cross-sectional area (1.43 × 10⁻³ m²)

Testing Results

Golf Ball: Experimental data showed an average error of 21% compared to theoretical values after curve fitting. Second-order polynomial regression achieved R² = 0.984, demonstrating excellent agreement. Dimples on the golf ball induced turbulence, resulting in lower drag forces at higher velocities compared to smooth sphere models.

3D-Printed Sphere: Smooth sphere testing yielded an average error of 10.4% with R² = 0.996. The superior correlation indicates the wind tunnel's ability to accurately measure aerodynamic forces in controlled conditions.

Challenges & Design Iterations

Challenge 1: Structural Integrity - Diffuser Collapse

Problem: The Alpha prototype cardboard diffuser collapsed under vacuum pressure created by the fan, compromising the structural geometry and creating back pressure in the system.

Solution: Redesigned the diffuser using rigid PolyWall material in a cylindrical geometry (9.25" to 16" diameter). The smooth cylindrical surface eliminated eddy formation points that plagued the cardboard frame supports. This design change improved structural integrity and flow quality simultaneously.

Challenge 2: Aerodynamic Flow Optimization

Problem: Internal wooden support frames in the Alpha diffuser created significant turbulence and eddies, causing inconsistent velocity profiles and inaccurate force measurements.

Solution: Implemented smooth curved transitions between components using 3D-printed adapter pieces with rounded corners. Replaced internal bracing with external supports on the PolyWall material. These changes successfully reduced turbulence and achieved laminar flow characteristics required for accurate aerodynamic testing.

Challenge 3: Testing Rod Design - Moment Arm Issues

Problem: The initial testing rod design with four straps created a moment arm that couldn't accurately counterbalance the golf ball, producing inaccurate drag force measurements in early testing phases.

Solution: Redesigned the rod assembly with improved moment arm geometry and rigid mounting. The refined design ensured zero moment about the pivot point, allowing the Vernier sensor to accurately capture drag forces without interference from gravitational moments.

Challenge 4: 3D Printer Size Constraints

Problem: Large adapter components exceeded the MakerBot 3D printer build platform dimensions, requiring creative solutions for multi-part assemblies.

Solution: Designed adapters using a "half & half" strategy—printing two identical half-pieces that are glued together using cyanoacrylate (Super Glue). This approach maintained structural integrity while accommodating printer limitations. The glue joint proved sufficiently strong for the low-stress adapter application.

The completed wind tunnel serves as a valuable resource for Doane University's engineering program, particularly supporting the EGR 310: Fluid Mechanics course. The system enables hands-on experimentation that reinforces theoretical concepts learned in the classroom.

Conclusion

This senior design project successfully delivered a functioning, educational wind tunnel that integrates fluid mechanics principles with hands-on engineering practice. Over three development phases, our team transformed initial concepts into a refined prototype capable of accurate aerodynamic measurements while maintaining affordability and accessibility for student use.

The iterative design process—from cardboard prototypes to precision-engineered components—demonstrated the importance of prototyping, testing, and refinement in engineering development. The final system achieves its primary objectives: providing students with experimental validation of theoretical fluid mechanics principles and establishing a foundation for future enhancements and research projects.

Lessons Learned

  • Design for Manufacturing: Understanding material properties and fabrication constraints early in design prevents costly late-stage modifications
  • Iterative Development: Testing at each phase gate identified critical issues early, allowing for strategic refinements rather than complete redesigns
  • Cross-Disciplinary Integration: Combining CAD design, manufacturing, electronics, and data analysis created a more robust final product
  • Team Communication: Regular meetings and documented progress ensured alignment and prevented rework
  • Documentation Value: Detailed SolidWorks models and testing procedures enable future student improvements and reduce the learning curve for new team members
